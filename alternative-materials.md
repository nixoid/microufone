# Inflatable Tethered Balloon — Alternative Materials Research

## Material Recommendation

The base fabric for inflatable balloons is almost always **nylon** (ripstop weave preferred — it stops small tears from propagating). What really matters is the **coating** applied to that nylon, because the coating is what makes it airtight. There are three options, and they differ enormously in how you build with them.

---

## Coating Options Explained

### 1. TPU (Thermoplastic Polyurethane) — best for DIY airtight construction

TPU is a **thermoplastic**: it softens when heated and re-hardens when cooled. This means two pieces of TPU-coated fabric can be **fused together with a household iron** (~200°C / 400°F) to form a permanent, airtight weld — no sewing, no glue. The weld is typically stronger than the fabric itself.

- **How to build with it:** Cut panels, overlap edges ~15mm, place parchment paper on top, press with a flat iron for 10–20 seconds per section, roll with a silicone roller while cooling. That's it — airtight seam.
- **Flexibility:** >500% elongation; stays pliable down to -40°C.
- **Durability:** Rated for >1 million flex cycles. High hydrolysis resistance (won't break down from moisture).
- **Repairability:** Patch with more TPU fabric + iron. Field-repairable.
- **Cost:** $6–30/yd depending on denier and supplier. Limited small-quantity US sources.
- **Catch:** Fewer US retail suppliers. The DIY packraft community is the main user base; most sources are specialty shops.

### 2. PU (Polyurethane) — cheaper, widely available, but NOT heat-sealable

PU is a **thermoset**: once cured, it cannot be re-melted. Two pieces of PU-coated fabric will **not** bond to each other with heat. This is the critical difference from TPU.

- **How to build with it:** Sew panels together, then seal every seam with **iron-on seam sealing tape** (PU-based tape, ~$10–15 for 100m on Amazon/Walmart). The tape is heat-activated and bonds to PU-coated surfaces, covering stitch holes. Alternatively, brush on liquid seam sealant (e.g., Gear Aid Seam Grip).
- **Flexibility:** 100–300% elongation. Good but less than TPU.
- **Durability:** ~100K flex cycles. Moderate hydrolysis resistance — can degrade in 1–2 years of heavy outdoor use.
- **Repairability:** Patch with tape or sealant. Slightly less convenient than TPU.
- **Cost:** $5–20/yd. Widely available from many US fabric retailers (Emmakites, Rockywoods, Canvas ETC).
- **Catch:** Airtightness depends entirely on the quality of your seam sealing. Every stitch hole is a potential leak. More labor-intensive than TPU welding.

### 3. PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride) — cheapest, heaviest, simplest to glue

PVC is a vinyl film, usually laminated onto polyester or nylon. It's the classic bouncy-house / inflatable-advertising material.

- **How to build with it:** Glue panels together with **HH-66 vinyl cement** or weld with a hot-air gun or RF welder. HH-66 is brush-on, sets in minutes, and is very forgiving.
- **Flexibility:** Stiff. Becomes brittle below 0°C. Plasticizers leach out over time making it progressively stiffer.
- **Durability:** Good tear/puncture resistance due to thick dense structure. But UV degrades it without protective coatings.
- **Repairability:** Easy — just glue a patch on. Vinyl cement is cheap and forgiving.
- **Cost:** $4–12/yd. Cheapest option. Wholesale rolls can be under $5/yd.
- **Catch:** Heavy (30–50% more than nylon equivalents). This means more tether stress, more drag in wind, and less payload capacity for glow hardware. Plasticizers degrade over time.

### 4. Dual-layer: TPU film bladder + uncoated ripstop shell — best for long-term flex resilience

Instead of using one coated fabric for both jobs, split them: a **pure TPU film** inner bladder holds the gas, and a separate **uncoated ripstop nylon** outer shell carries shape, mounting hardware, UV/abrasion protection, and visual styling. The two layers are mechanically independent — no adhesion, no welding between them — so the failure mode that kills coated fabrics over time is eliminated.

- **Why this exists:** Practitioner feedback (Armand Matossian, who has built inflatables professionally) reports that **TPU coatings delaminate from ripstop** under repeated inflation/deflation and wind-flex cycling. Once delamination starts, the fabric balloons locally where the coating has lifted, leaks, and concentrates further stress. He has had much better long-term results splitting the layers and using straight TPU film as a separate gas-holding bladder. American Polyfilm's [TPU film for inflatables and advertising](https://www.americanpolyfilm.com/inflatables-and-advertising-tpu-film) line is one of the canonical sources for that bladder film.
- **How to build with it:**
  - **Bladder:** cut TPU film panels, overlap edges ~15 mm, iron-weld through parchment (~200°C) — same procedure as TPU-coated fabric, just with a film both sides instead of a fabric/film composite.
  - **Shell:** cut uncoated ripstop panels, sew them with a domestic machine; no seam tape, no welding, no airtightness needed because the shell never sees pressure.
  - **Assembly:** install Boston valve on the bladder; cut a Velcro-closed access flap on the shell over the valve; drop the deflated bladder inside the shell; inflate through the access flap.
- **Flexibility:** identical to single-wall TPU on the bladder; full ripstop range on the shell (no temperature-driven coating brittleness because there's no coating).
- **Durability:** addresses the dominant long-term failure mode of coated fabric (delamination). Bladder and shell can each be replaced or upgraded independently.
- **Repairability:** patch bladder with iron-welded TPU film; patch shell by sewing on more ripstop or with iron-on repair tape. Either layer can be swapped without rebuilding the other.
- **Cost:** roughly the sum of two layers (~$10–25/yd film + $5–15/yd uncoated ripstop). Comparable per-yard to mid-range single-wall TPU, but you buy two layers' worth of material.
- **Catch:** more parts and assembly steps than single-wall. Two cuts per panel. Once the shell is on, the bladder is harder to inspect — leaks must be diagnosed by deflation behavior or by removing the bladder. Adds a shell access port for valve service.

## Recommendation

For a 2–3m tethered balloon in moderate-to-strong wind with glow hardware hanging off it:

- **Dual-layer (TPU film bladder + uncoated ripstop shell)** is the recommended final architecture, and is the selection captured in [`specs.md`](specs.md). It avoids the TPU-from-ripstop delamination failure mode that practitioners report on long-term outdoor builds, mechanically isolates the gas envelope from all tether/ring/light loads, and lets either layer be replaced or upgraded without rebuilding the other. Trades simplicity and a bit of weight for resilience.
- **TPU-coated nylon ripstop** (single-wall) is the simplest viable final material if you accept a shorter flex-cycle lifetime and want one fabric to do everything. Lightest overall and easiest to weld. Good for a one-event build or where coated-fabric delamination is unlikely to be reached.
- **PU-coated nylon ripstop** is a great prototyping material — cheap, widely available, and good enough to test your panel geometry and inflation behavior. Just accept that seam-sealing is fiddly and you may get slow leaks.
- **PVC** is fine for a first "does this shape even work?" throwaway prototype where you don't care about weight or longevity.

## Suggested Approach for Iterative Prototyping

1. **Prototype round 1 (PU, sew + seal)** — Buy 10–20 yards of **40D PU-coated ripstop** from **Emmakites** (~$50–80). Sew panels, seal seams with iron-on PU seam tape ($10–15 on Amazon for 100m). Goal: test your panel geometry and inflation shape as a single-wall throwaway. Accept some slow leaks.
2. **Prototype round 2 (dual-layer, layers tested separately)** — Once your pattern works, build the bladder and shell independently:
   - Bladder: **TPU film** from **American Polyfilm** (or DIY Packraft / Extremtextil), iron-welded, leak-tested standalone with air for ≥24 h.
   - Shell: **uncoated 40D ripstop** from **Ripstop by the Roll** or **Emmakites**, sewn, with attachment patches and a Velcro access port over the valve location.
   - Integrated assembly: drop bladder into shell, inflate, verify retention and that no shell load ever reaches the bladder.
3. **Final build** — Scale to full size. If the bladder permeates too fast, step up TPU film thickness (e.g. 4 mil → 6 mil). If shell wind-deforms, step up to heavier ripstop or add reinforcement patches. Bulk material via **Alibaba** if building multiple copies.
4. **Glow integration** — Test LED strip / EL wire attachment on the **shell** only — sewn patches, no bladder modifications ever.
5. **Local consult** — Reach out to **INFLATABILL** for construction advice; they're Bay Area–based and specialize in exactly this kind of festival inflatable art.

## Inflation Port: How to Fill and Seal

You need a way to get helium into the balloon and then close it airtight. The approach depends on your fabric type. In all cases, the valve is installed **during construction** — not cut into a finished balloon.

### Option A: Boston Valve (recommended for TPU fabric)

A Boston valve is a two-part plastic check valve (~44g) with a wide threaded base and a screw cap. It's the standard for packrafts, inflatable boats, and air mattresses. It has a one-way flap inside so gas doesn't escape while you're connecting/disconnecting the fill hose.

**How to install on TPU fabric:**

1. Pick your valve location — ideally near the bottom/back of the balloon where it won't interfere with the visual design or glow elements.
2. Mark and cut a **4 cm circle** in the fabric panel (do this before you close the balloon — it's much easier to work flat).
3. Push the valve body through from the inside so the flange sits flat against the interior surface. The three raised alignment bumps face inward.
4. Cut a **reinforcement ring** from TPU fabric scrap: ~12 cm outer diameter, 4 cm center hole.
5. Place the ring over the valve flange on the outside, TPU side down against the balloon skin.
6. Cover with parchment paper and iron-weld from the center outward, 6–8 seconds per section. Work outward to push air bubbles toward the edges.
7. Remove parchment, let cool, check the bond by gently tugging the ring.

**To fill:** Unscrew the top cap, attach a helium regulator hose (standard inflator nozzle fits), fill to desired pressure. The one-way flap holds gas when you remove the hose.

**To top up:** Same process — the check valve prevents loss while you reconnect.

**To deflate:** Unscrew the middle section to expose a wide opening for fast gas release.

### Option B: Primitive Fabric Valve (simplest, no hardware)

If you don't want to buy a valve or want something lighter, you can make a one-way flap valve entirely from TPU fabric scraps. This is the lightest option and has no rigid parts.

**How to build:**

1. Cut two rectangles of TPU fabric, ~5 cm × 10 cm.
2. Stack them TPU-side-to-TPU-side. Insert a strip of parchment paper or silnylon between them to prevent bonding in the center channel.
3. Iron-weld one long edge shut (~0.5 cm seam allowance). The parchment keeps the middle open.
4. Iron-weld the other long edge shut the same way.
5. You now have a flat tube that's sealed on both long sides but open at both short ends.
6. Weld one short end into a seam on your balloon panel during construction — this is the interior opening.
7. The other short end is the exterior fill port.
8. Remove the parchment strip from inside.

**To fill:** Insert the helium nozzle or a plastic straw into the tube, fill the balloon. When you remove the nozzle, internal pressure presses the two fabric layers together, sealing the tube flat like a check valve.

**To seal permanently:** Once fully inflated, iron-weld the exterior opening shut (press flat, parchment on top, iron for 6–8 seconds). This is a permanent seal — you'll need to cut it open to deflate and re-weld to re-seal.

**Trade-offs vs. Boston valve:** Lighter, cheaper (free if you have TPU scraps), no hardware to buy. But less convenient for topping up — a permanent weld means you can't easily re-inflate if the balloon slowly loses helium over a multi-day festival.

### Option C: Glued Fill Tube (for PVC fabric)

PVC can't be heat-welded at home with a simple iron, so valves are glued in.

1. Cut a short tube (~8 cm long, 3–4 cm diameter) from PVC fabric, or use a section of rigid PVC pipe wrapped in fabric.
2. Glue it into a hole in the balloon panel using **HH-66 vinyl cement**. Apply cement to both surfaces, wait 30 seconds until tacky, press together, smooth from center outward.
3. After filling with helium, fold the tube flat and clamp or tie it off with a cable tie, then glue the fold shut with another pass of HH-66.
4. Allow 45+ minutes cure time before any stress on the joint.

**Recommendation for your use case:** Install a **Boston valve** on your TPU final build. At a multi-day outdoor festival, helium will slowly permeate through any fabric, and you'll want to top up without cutting the balloon open. For early PU/PVC prototypes, a primitive valve or glued tube is fine since those are throwaway builds anyway.
